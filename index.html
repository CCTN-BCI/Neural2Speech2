Natural speech re-synthesis from direct cortical recordings utilizing pre-trained acoustic and linguistic speech generators

Abstract: Reconstructing perceived speech stimuli from neural recordings is not only advancing the understanding of the neural coding underlying speech processing but also an important building block for brain-computer interfaces and neuroprosthetics. However, prior methods predominantly focused on acoustic reconstruction while neglecting linguistic intelligibility, fundamentally limiting clinical utility. In order to overcome this limitation, we proposed a dual-path encoder-decoder framework that concurrently decodes acoustic and linguistic representations. In this work, we proposed a dual-path pre-trained encoder-decoder framework to address these problems. We recorded high-density electrocorticography (ECoG) signals when participants listening to natural speech. Our architecture integrates: an acoustic pathway consists of an LSTM adaptor and a HiFi-GAN generator to decode spectral features, and a linguistic pathway consists of a transformer adaptor and a text-to-speech (TTS) generator to decode word tokens and finally merge them by timbre cloning for speaker identity preservation. Using only 20 minutes of ECoG data from a single participant, we achieve significant intelligibility of the neural-driven re-synthesized natural speech (MOS = 3.956 ± 0.173, word error rate = 17.7% ± 3.2%). This demonstrates that integrating acoustic and linguistic adaptors with timbre cloning after neural feature alignment enables efficient decoding of naturally intelligible speech—paving the way for clinically viable neuroprosthetics.
